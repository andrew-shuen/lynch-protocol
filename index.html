01

Understanding the conditions

What this means for you

Lynch syndrome is an inherited condition that raises your lifetime chance of certain cancers, most often of the bowel and womb, and tends to cause them at younger ages. The exact risks depend on which gene is involved, so your screening plan is tailored to your specific gene. Regular check-ups, a daily medication for some people, risk-reducing surgery where appropriate, and everyday habits can lower that risk substantially and catch anything early, when it is most treatable.

Lynch syndrome

Lynch syndrome is a hereditary cancer predisposition syndrome caused by germline mutations in one of the DNA mismatch repair (MMR) genes — MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 — or deletions in the EPCAM gene that lead to silencing of MSH2. The MMR system corrects errors arising during DNA replication, such as base mismatches and insertion-deletion loops. When this repair is impaired by a pathogenic variant, errors accumulate, particularly in repetitive sequences known as microsatellites, producing microsatellite instability (MSI). This genomic instability promotes carcinogenesis.

Critically, lifetime cancer risk differs substantially by gene. MLH1 and MSH2 carry the highest risks; MSH6 confers a high endometrial risk but lower and later-onset colorectal risk; and PMS2 carries the lowest risks of the four. Surveillance should therefore be gene-specific rather than uniform, and the sections below are organized accordingly.

CMMRD

Constitutional mismatch repair deficiency (CMMRD) is a rare disorder caused by biallelic pathogenic variants in the MMR genes (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2). It produces defective mismatch repair in all tissues, with genomic instability and a markedly increased cancer risk. Affected individuals typically develop cancers in childhood or early adulthood (colorectal, brain, and hematological malignancies such as leukemia and lymphoma) among others. Penetrance approaches 100%. CMMRD requires a distinct, intensive pediatric-onset surveillance protocol that is beyond the scope of this adult-focused document and should be managed in a specialized centre.

03

MLH1-associated Lynch syndrome

MLH1

Highest-risk group, alongside MSH2

CRC 40–60% · Endometrial 35–55%

Your screening at a glance

MLH1 carries one of the highest cancer risks, so screening is the most intensive. The cornerstone is colonoscopy beginning in your early twenties and repeated every one to two years. Women also plan for risk-reducing gynecologic surgery after childbearing (covered in its own section).

  • Colorectal: colonoscopy from age 20–25 (or 2–5 years before the youngest family case), every 1–2 years. Consider total colectomy for cancer or adenomas not manageable endoscopically. [1, 2]
  • Endometrial / ovarian: education on abnormal bleeding; risk-reducing hysterectomy + BSO after childbearing (see surgery section). TVUS/biopsy optional and low-yield. [1, 2]
  • Gastric / small bowel: EGD with gastric biopsy for H. pylori at 30–35, every 2–4 years; consider duodenoscopy with small-bowel family history. [3]
  • Pancreatic: only if a first-degree relative is affected — annual MRCP or EUS from age 50 (or 10 years before the youngest case). [1, 2]
  • Prostate (males): annual PSA from age 40. [1, 2]
  • Urinary tract: risk lower than MSH2 (0.2–4%). NCCN notes insufficient evidence for surveillance; optional annual urinalysis from 30–35 may be considered, otherwise investigate symptoms. [1, 2]
  • Skin (Muir-Torre): sebaceous neoplasms and keratoacanthomas occur in Lynch syndrome (the Muir-Torre phenotype), with MLH1 among the implicated genes. Consider an annual total-body skin examination, with biopsy and referral for any sebaceous adenoma/carcinoma or keratoacanthoma. Evidence on optimal interval is limited; this follows expert consensus. [1, 2]
  • Brain: no routine imaging; investigate neurologic symptoms. [1, 2]
04

MSH2/EPCAM-associated Lynch syndrome

MSH2 / EPCAM

Highest-risk group; the urinary-tract gene

CRC 40–60% · Urinary up to 28%

Your screening at a glance

MSH2 carries cancer risks as high as MLH1, with one important addition: it is the gene most associated with urinary-tract cancer, so urine-based screening is considered for MSH2 carriers specifically. Colonoscopy remains the cornerstone, and women plan for risk-reducing gynecologic surgery after childbearing.

  • Colorectal: colonoscopy from age 20–25 (or 2–5 years before the youngest family case), every 1–2 years. Consider total colectomy if not manageable endoscopically. [1, 2]
  • Endometrial / ovarian: education on abnormal bleeding; risk-reducing hysterectomy + BSO after childbearing (see surgery section). TVUS/biopsy optional and low-yield. [1, 2]
  • Gastric / small bowel: EGD with gastric biopsy for H. pylori at 30–35, every 2–4 years; consider duodenoscopy with small-bowel family history. [3]
  • Urinary tract: highest in MSH2 (4–28%, upper tract). NCCN notes no clear evidence to support surveillance but lists optional annual urinalysis from age 30–35 as a surveillance option; this is a shared decision given low test sensitivity and no proven mortality benefit. Cytology or upper-tract imaging for symptoms or strong family history. [1, 2]
  • Pancreatic: only if a first-degree relative is affected — annual MRCP or EUS from age 50 (or 10 years before the youngest case). [1, 2]
  • Prostate (males): annual PSA from age 40. [1, 2]
  • Skin (Muir-Torre): MSH2 is the gene most strongly associated with the Muir-Torre phenotype (sebaceous adenomas, sebaceous carcinomas, keratoacanthomas). Recommend an annual total-body skin examination, with low threshold to biopsy and refer suspicious lesions. Evidence on optimal interval is limited; this follows expert consensus. [1, 2]
  • Brain: no routine imaging; investigate neurologic symptoms. [1, 2]
05

MSH6-associated Lynch syndrome

MSH6

Endometrial-predominant; lower, later colorectal risk

Endometrial 16–49% · CRC 20–44%

Your screening at a glance

MSH6 behaves differently from MLH1 and MSH2: the endometrial (womb) risk is high, while bowel cancer risk is lower and tends to appear later. Screening reflects this — colonoscopy can often start a little later and gynecologic awareness is especially important.

  • Colorectal: per NCCN, colonoscopy from age 30–35 (or 2–5 years before the youngest family case if diagnosed before 30), every 1–3 years, reflecting lower and later-onset risk than MLH1/MSH2. [1, 2]
  • Endometrial / ovarian: the predominant risk — strong education on abnormal bleeding (which usually presents early and curable); risk-reducing hysterectomy + BSO after childbearing (see surgery section). [1, 2]
  • Gastric / small bowel: no routine surveillance unless a strong family history (risk <1%). [1, 2]
  • Urinary tract: no routine surveillance (rare). [1, 2]
  • Skin (Muir-Torre): lower association than MSH2/MLH1. No intensive schedule mandated; maintain awareness and biopsy/refer any sebaceous neoplasm or keratoacanthoma. A periodic skin check is reasonable. [1, 2]
  • Pancreatic / prostate / brain: no routine gene-driven surveillance; manage by family history and symptoms. Prostate PSA per general high-risk discussion. [1, 2]
06

PMS2-associated Lynch syndrome

PMS2

Lowest-risk group of the four genes

CRC 10–20% · Endometrial 13–26%

A lower-risk gene

PMS2 carries the most modest cancer risk of the four Lynch genes, so the plan is lighter. Colonoscopy remains central, but most of the additional organ screens are not routinely needed unless your family history points to them.

  • Colorectal: per NCCN, colonoscopy from age 30–35 (or 2–5 years before the youngest family case if diagnosed before 30), every 1–3 years. [1, 2]
  • Endometrial: education on abnormal uterine bleeding, with endometrial biopsy to evaluate symptoms; routine TVUS/biopsy screening not mandated. Risk-reducing hysterectomy considered on an individualized basis after childbearing (see surgery section). [1, 2]
  • Ovarian: NCCN notes PMS2 carriers appear to be at no greater than average ovarian cancer risk; surveillance and risk-reducing oophorectomy may reasonably be deferred. [1, 2]
  • Gastric / small bowel: no routine surveillance unless strong family history. [1, 2]
  • Urinary tract: no routine surveillance unless family history. [1, 2]
  • Skin (Muir-Torre): low association. No routine schedule; maintain awareness and biopsy/refer any sebaceous neoplasm or keratoacanthoma. [1, 2]
  • Pancreatic / prostate / brain: no routine gene-driven surveillance; manage by family history and symptoms. [1, 2]

Risk-reducing surgery & hormone replacement therapy

Surgery and hormones, together

For women with Lynch syndrome, removing the uterus and ovaries after childbearing is the most effective way to prevent womb and ovarian cancer. If the ovaries are removed before natural menopause, hormone replacement therapy protects your heart, bones, and quality of life — and reassuringly does not raise bowel cancer risk in Lynch syndrome. These two decisions are planned together.

Prophylactic hysterectomy and bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy (BSO)

Risk-reducing hysterectomy with BSO is the most effective intervention against endometrial and ovarian cancer in Lynch syndrome, and unlike surveillance it has a clear preventive effect. Timing and strength of recommendation are gene-dependent.

MLH1, MSH2/EPCAM Recommend hysterectomy + BSO after completion of childbearing, typically ages 40–45 (individualized), given high endometrial and meaningful ovarian risk. Counsel on surgical menopause and HRT before surgery.
MSH6 Hysterectomy is reasonable after childbearing given high endometrial risk. NCCN notes insufficient evidence to make a specific recommendation for risk-reducing BSO in MSH6; otherwise guidance mirrors MLH1/MSH2. Individualize the ovarian decision.
PMS2 NCCN notes PMS2 carriers appear at no greater than average ovarian cancer risk and may reasonably elect against oophorectomy and defer surveillance; hysterectomy is individualized. For those who do opt for surgery/surveillance, guidance mirrors MLH1/MSH2. Decide with the patient and genetics input.

Hormone replacement therapy after surgical menopause

Premature estrogen loss after BSO before natural menopause increases cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, cognitive decline, and quality-of-life burden. Current evidence indicates HRT does not increase colorectal cancer risk (the primary Lynch-associated cancer) and may be protective. Estrogen-alone therapy is preferred after hysterectomy; combined estrogen-progestin is needed only if a uterus remains. HRT is individualized by age, time since surgery, and personal and family cancer history.

No personal cancer history Recommend HRT after premenopausal BSO, especially under age 50, to manage symptoms and protect bone and cardiovascular health. Typically continue to the natural menopause age (~50–51 years).
Personal cancer history Individualize by cancer type, stage, histology, and recurrence risk, with oncology input. Early-stage, low-risk endometrial or ovarian cancers may permit cautious HRT; avoid in hormone-sensitive cancers (e.g. breast) unless benefits clearly outweigh risks. Non-hormonal symptom management is preferred where HRT is contraindicated.

Aspirin chemoprevention

A daily tablet that lowers risk

A low daily dose of aspirin reduces bowel cancer risk in Lynch syndrome across all the genes. It is not right for everyone — the main trade-off is a small increase in bleeding risk — so your clinician weighs the benefit against your personal bleeding risk before recommending it, and reviews it each year.

Background

Aspirin reduces colorectal cancer risk through several mechanisms, chiefly inhibition of cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, particularly COX-2, which drive inflammation and are often overexpressed in colorectal tumors. It may also induce apoptosis, inhibit proliferation, and impair platelet-mediated tumor growth.

The CAPP2 trial in Lynch syndrome first showed that aspirin 600 mg daily for at least 2 years significantly lowers the incidence of colorectal and other Lynch-associated cancers, an effect that did not become apparent until about 5 years from commencement and persisted for over a decade. The dose-finding question was then addressed by the CaPP3 non-inferiority trial (reported 2025), comparing 75–100 mg, 300 mg, and 600 mg daily: the lowest dose was as effective as the higher doses with fewer bleeding and gastrointestinal adverse events. Low-dose aspirin is therefore now the preferred chemopreventive dose. Major guidelines advise that Lynch carriers consider daily aspirin; the specific low-dose figure is supported by CaPP3 and being incorporated into formal recommendations rather than long-codified. Risk–benefit evaluation is essential before initiating, particularly for gastrointestinal bleeding.

Recommendation Recommend low-dose aspirin for colorectal and Lynch-associated cancer risk reduction across MLH1, MSH2/EPCAM, MSH6, and PMS2. In Canada the available low-dose formulation is 81 mg (equivalent to the 75–100 mg range studied in CaPP3); 100 mg tablets are not a standard Canadian retail dose. Begin in the mid-20s to 30s if tolerated. Reassess at least annually for gastrointestinal and bleeding risk. Because the benefit emerges only after roughly 5 years and bleeding risk rises with both treatment duration and age (especially over 65), review aspirin periodically as the patient ages and treatment lengthens; the strongest current evidence covers a roughly 2–5 year horizon, and longer-term continuation can be re-evaluated as extended follow-up data mature. [3]

Assessing bleeding risk

Low risk
  • Age <70
  • No prior GI bleed or peptic ulcer
  • No hemorrhagic stroke or bleeding disorder
  • Not on anticoagulants / multiple antiplatelets
  • Normal CBC, liver and kidney function
Moderate risk
  • Age 70–75
  • Prior ulcer/gastritis, no recent GI bleed
  • One added risk med (NSAID, low-dose AC)
  • Mild thrombocytopenia or mild–moderate CKD (uremic platelet dysfunction raises bleeding risk)
  • Uncontrolled hypertension (elevated hemorrhagic stroke risk; control BP before/while on antiplatelet)
  • Consider PPI co-prescription; monitor closely
High risk
  • Age >75 with frailty
  • Major GI bleed or hemorrhagic stroke
  • Active peptic ulcer or cirrhosis
  • Plt <100, Hb <100 g/L, or INR >1.5 off AC
  • Anticoagulation + multiple risk meds

Bleeding risk calculator

NZ Aspirin Benefit-Harm Calculator

The New Zealand Aspirin Benefit-Harm Calculator (Selak et al.) estimates an individual’s 5-year risk of a first cardiovascular event and of a major bleed, both with and without aspirin, quantifying the net benefit or harm of aspirin for primary prevention to support shared decision-making. It is derived from sex-specific Cox proportional hazards models built on a contemporary New Zealand primary-care cohort of over 385,000 people without established cardiovascular disease, and it accounts for concurrent medications and comorbidities. Note that the underlying data are New Zealand-based and may not fully generalize elsewhere.

aspirinbenefitharmcalculator.shinyapps.io/calculator

  • Net harm — excess major bleeds outweigh prevented CV events: caution or avoid aspirin.
  • Net neutral / marginal — shared decision-making, weighing the Lynch-specific CRC chemoprevention benefit alongside the CV estimate.
  • Net benefit — prevented CV events outweigh excess bleeds: aspirin generally favorable.

Because this tool estimates cardiovascular benefit-harm rather than cancer-specific benefit, in Lynch syndrome the colorectal cancer risk reduction should be considered an additional benefit on top of the calculator output when making the final recommendation.

Genetic counselling

Sharing this with family

Because Lynch syndrome runs in families, your relatives may benefit from testing too — called cascade testing. Counselling also covers family-planning options if you wish to explore them, and keeping your family history up to date so your plan stays right for you over time.

Offer genetic counselling at diagnosis and to all at-risk relatives (cascade testing). Discuss reproductive options including PGT-M for known pathogenic variants. Discuss partner carrier testing for the risk of constitutional mismatch repair deficiency (CMMRD). Maintain and update family pedigrees, and reassess risk if new cancers occur or family history evolves. [1, 2]

Lifestyle & nutrition

Everyday prevention

What you eat and how you live genuinely move the needle. A Mediterranean-style diet, regular movement, good sleep, no tobacco, avoiding alcohol, and a few well-chosen supplements all lower bowel cancer risk and support overall health.

Adopt a Mediterranean-style diet rich in vegetables, fruits, legumes, nuts, healthy fats (especially olive oil), and fish. Emphasize plant-based whole foods and probiotic-rich foods such as fermented vegetables, yogurt, kefir, and kombucha.

Avoid dietary carcinogens: red meat, processed meats with nitrates/nitrites, and smoked, barbecued, grilled, fried, or heavily baked foods. Avoid added sugars, sugar-sweetened beverages, and refined carbohydrates. Avoid alcohol, which is an established risk factor for colorectal and several other cancers; there is no clearly safe threshold, so abstinence is the goal.

Maintain optimal body composition. Circadian optimization: encourage early time-restricted feeding (10–12 h window, e.g. 8 AM–6 PM); consider a 6–8 h window if weight loss is a goal. [14-17] Engage in at least 150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity exercise. Avoid tobacco. Prioritize sleep and stress management.

Supplements

  • Omega-3 fatty acids — 2–3 g/day EPA + DHA; measure baseline omega-3 index. [18]
  • Vitamin D & calcium — target serum 25(OH)D of 125 nmol/L (50 ng/mL); total calcium 1000–1200 mg/day, calcium citrate if diet insufficient.
  • Fiber — at least 25–35 g/day to reduce colorectal cancer risk.

Microbiome & gut health

The bacteria in your gut

The mix of bacteria living in your bowel can influence cancer risk. Treating a stomach bug called H. pylori, eating more fibre and fermented foods, and good oral hygiene all help tip the balance toward protective bacteria.

Background

The gut microbiome influences colorectal cancer development via inflammation, immune regulation, and metabolism of dietary and host-derived compounds. Dysbiosis is associated with increased pro-carcinogenic species such as Fusobacterium nucleatum, Escherichia coli (harboring the pks genomic island), and enterotoxigenic Bacteroides fragilis, which can promote DNA damage, chronic inflammation, and immune evasion. A reduction in beneficial short-chain fatty-acid producers like Faecalibacterium prausnitzii may impair mucosal barrier integrity. These associations are increasingly recognized as biomarkers of risk and potential prevention targets.

Recommendation — H. pylori (the one clinically available test) Test for H. pylori every 2–4 years as part of upper-GI surveillance and treat confirmed infection. Two 14-day first-line regimens are used: clarithromycin triple therapy (clarithromycin, amoxicillin, PPI) and bismuth quadruple therapy (bismuth, metronidazole, tetracycline, PPI). Because rising clarithromycin resistance (now >30% in many regions) has eroded triple-therapy efficacy to roughly 75–85%, while optimized bismuth quadruple therapy achieves higher eradication (about 85–95%), the 2024 ACG guidelines now favor bismuth quadruple therapy as the preferred first-line option, particularly where resistance is high or unknown. Confirm eradication at least 4 weeks after antibiotics (and 2 weeks after stopping the PPI) using a stool antigen test or urea breath test rather than serology.

A note on microbiome testing

Several gut microbes have been linked in research to colorectal cancer — including pks⁺ Escherichia coli (produces the genotoxin colibactin), enterotoxigenic Bacteroides fragilis, and Fusobacterium nucleatum. However, there is no validated clinical test for these organisms at this time, and detecting them does not yet have a proven, actionable management pathway. Some private or direct-to-consumer microbiome tests may report on them, but these are not clinically validated for cancer risk. Patients interested in such testing should discuss the options, and their limitations, with their physician. By contrast, H. pylori testing is clinically available and validated, and is the only microbiome-related test currently recommended here.

General gut-supportive measures remain reasonable regardless of testing: increase dietary fiber, include prebiotic and polyphenol-rich foods, maintain good oral hygiene, and use antibiotics judiciously.

Patients with cancer: microbiome & immunotherapy

If you are being treated for cancer

Lynch-related cancers often respond very well to immunotherapy. There is growing evidence that a healthy gut microbiome can help immunotherapy work better. While this is still an evolving area, supporting your gut with fibre, fermented foods, and avoiding unnecessary antibiotics is reasonable and safe alongside your cancer treatment.

Why this matters in Lynch syndrome

Lynch-associated cancers are typically mismatch-repair-deficient and microsatellite-instability-high (MSI-H), a phenotype that is strongly responsive to immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) such as anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 therapy. Because many patients with Lynch syndrome who develop cancer will be candidates for immunotherapy, the microbiome’s influence on ICI efficacy is particularly relevant to this population.

The gut microbiome modulates the immune response to cancer and influences ICI efficacy. Commensals including Akkermansia muciniphila, Bifidobacterium species, and Faecalibacterium prausnitzii are associated with improved ICI responses by enhancing antigen presentation, promoting T-cell infiltration, and modulating systemic inflammation. Conversely, dysbiosis or recent broad-spectrum antibiotics can blunt efficacy. Fecal microbiota transplantation from responders into germ-free mice restores checkpoint sensitivity, supporting a causative role. Probiotics, prebiotics, diet, and FMT are under active investigation as adjuncts.

Practical optimization before / during immunotherapy Favor a high-fiber, plant-forward diet and probiotic-rich fermented foods (yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, kombucha). Support beneficial taxa such as Akkermansia muciniphila through dietary fiber and polyphenol-rich foods. Avoid unnecessary antibiotics, which can reduce microbial diversity and may impair ICI response. Coordinate with the treating oncology team.

What is and isn't established

Dietary and antibiotic-stewardship measures are low-risk and reasonable. Targeted interventions such as specific probiotic supplements or FMT to enhance immunotherapy are investigational and should occur within clinical trials or specialist guidance, not as routine care. There is no validated clinical microbiome test to direct these choices at present.

Emerging & investigational prevention

On the horizon

Research is actively exploring new ways to prevent Lynch-related cancers. The approaches below are promising but not yet standard care. Ask your physician whether any current clinical trials might be a fit for you.

Cancer-preventive vaccines

Because MMR-deficient tumors generate predictable shared neoantigens (from frameshift peptides), vaccine strategies aim to prime the immune system to recognize and eliminate pre-cancerous cells in Lynch carriers. Early-phase trials of frameshift-peptide vaccines are underway; this is investigational and trial-only at present.

Optimizing aspirin and other agents

The CaPP3 trial refined aspirin dosing toward the lowest effective dose. Ongoing work addresses optimal duration and whether other anti-inflammatory or metabolic agents add benefit. Naproxen and other NSAIDs have been studied as alternative chemopreventives in early trials.

Microbiome-directed prevention

Modulating pro-carcinogenic microbes (e.g. pks⁺ E. coli, enterotoxigenic B. fragilis, F. nucleatum) through diet, targeted antimicrobials, or live biotherapeutics is an active research area. No validated clinical test or intervention exists yet; this remains investigational.

Omega-3 and nutritional chemoprevention

The COLYNE trial is evaluating moderate-dose omega-3 (EPA + DHA) for colorectal cancer prevention specifically in Lynch syndrome. Results will clarify whether nutritional supplementation has a defined preventive role. [18]

None of the above should replace established screening, chemoprevention, or risk-reducing surgery. Patients interested in investigational options should be referred to or discuss enrollment in appropriate clinical trials.

Summary of recommendations

The whole plan on one page

Screening is summarized below by gene, followed by the cross-cutting recommendations that apply across Lynch syndrome.

Screening by Lynch subtype

MLH1

Colon: colonoscopy from 20–25, q1–2y.

Gyne: education + risk-reducing hysterectomy/BSO after childbearing (35–45); TVUS/biopsy optional.

Gastric: EGD + H. pylori at 30–35, q2–4y.

Pancreas: if FDR affected, from 50. Prostate: PSA from 40.

Skin: annual total-body skin exam (Muir-Torre).

Urinary/brain: no routine screening.

MSH2 / EPCAM

Colon: colonoscopy from 20–25, q1–2y.

Gyne: education + risk-reducing hysterectomy/BSO after childbearing (35–45); TVUS/biopsy optional.

Gastric: EGD + H. pylori at 30–35, q2–4y.

Urinary: optional, evidence-limited annual urinalysis from 30–35 (this gene specifically).

Pancreas: if FDR affected, from 50. Prostate: PSA from 40.

Skin: annual total-body skin exam — highest Muir-Torre risk.

MSH6

Colon: colonoscopy from 30–35, q1–3y (NCCN; lower, later risk).

Gyne: predominant risk — strong bleeding education + risk-reducing hysterectomy/BSO after childbearing.

Gastric/urinary: no routine screening unless family history.

Skin/other: skin awareness, biopsy sebaceous lesions; otherwise by family history and symptoms.

PMS2

Colon: colonoscopy from 30–35, q1–3y (NCCN).

Gyne: education + biopsy for symptoms; hysterectomy individualized. At no greater than average ovarian risk — may defer oophorectomy/surveillance.

Gastric/urinary/other: no routine screening unless strong family history. Skin: awareness, biopsy sebaceous lesions.

Cross-cutting recommendations

Aspirin chemoprevention

Low-dose aspirin from mid-20s to 30s if tolerated; in Canada use 81 mg/day (per CaPP3 non-inferiority).

Assess CV benefit and bleeding harm at least annually with the NZ calculator, adding the Lynch-specific CRC benefit. Reassess with age and duration; strongest evidence ~2–5 y. Avoid in high bleeding risk.

Risk-reducing surgery & HRT

Hysterectomy + BSO after childbearing for MLH1/MSH2. MSH6: hysterectomy reasonable; BSO evidence insufficient (NCCN). PMS2: individualized; may defer oophorectomy.

HRT after premenopausal BSO (estrogen-only if no uterus) unless hormone-sensitive cancer; individualize with oncology if prior cancer.

Genetic counselling

Offer at diagnosis and to at-risk relatives (cascade testing).

Discuss PGT-M, partner carrier testing (CMMRD risk), and maintain the pedigree.

Microbiome

H. pylori testing q2–4y is the only clinically available microbiome test; eradicate if positive.

No validated clinical test for pks⁺ E. coli, ETBF, or F. nucleatum; private tests are unvalidated — discuss with physician.

Lifestyle, diet & supplements

Mediterranean diet; avoid red/processed meats, added sugars, alcohol. Early time-restricted feeding; exercise ≥150 min/week; no tobacco.

Omega-3 2–3 g/day; vitamin D target 125 nmol/L; calcium 1000–1200 mg/day; fiber 25–35 g/day.

Emerging / investigational

Frameshift-peptide preventive vaccines, microbiome-directed prevention, and omega-3 (COLYNE) are under study.

Trial-only; do not replace established care. Refer interested patients to clinical trials.

Patients with cancer — immunotherapy & microbiome

Lynch cancers are typically MSI-H and highly responsive to immune checkpoint inhibitors. Support the gut microbiome with a high-fiber, plant-forward diet and fermented foods, and avoid unnecessary antibiotics, which may impair response. Targeted probiotics or FMT to enhance immunotherapy are investigational and trial-only. Coordinate with oncology.
